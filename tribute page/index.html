A Tribute to

Dr. APJ Abdul Kalam

15 October 1931 — 27 July 2015

Scientist. President. Dreamer.
The man who taught a nation to aim for the stars.

A Life Worth Knowing

Avul Pakir Jainulabdeen Abdul Kalam was born in Rameswaram, a small island town in Tamil Nadu, into a modest family. His father was a boat owner who ferried Hindu pilgrims across to Dhanushkodi. There wasn't much money, but there was a lot of discipline and a deep sense of community. Kalam delivered newspapers in the morning to help pay for his own education. He'd talk about those years not with bitterness, but with something closer to gratitude — like difficulty had done him a favour.

He trained as an aerospace engineer at MIT Chennai and later joined ISRO and then DRDO, where he spent decades working on India's space and missile programmes. He was the project director for India's first Satellite Launch Vehicle — SLV-III — which successfully deployed the Rohini satellite in 1980. Later, he led the development of the Agni and Prithvi missiles under India's Integrated Guided Missile Development Programme. Colleagues described him as the kind of person who stayed late not because he had to, but because he genuinely could not imagine being anywhere else.

What made Kalam unusual among scientists of his generation was how much time he spent with young people. He gave lectures at colleges, replied to student letters, and later wrote several books aimed not at researchers but at teenagers trying to figure out what they wanted from life. Wings of Fire, his autobiography, has sold millions of copies and is still assigned reading in schools across India. It reads less like a career retrospective and more like a long conversation with someone who genuinely believed in you.

He served as India's 11th President from 2002 to 2007 — a role he is said to have accepted with some reluctance, not out of lack of patriotism but because he wasn't sure a President could do more good than a scientist. He was wrong, or at least, he was different kinds of right. He used the position to visit remote schools, meet children in hospitals, and push for science education in places that had been left behind. When his term ended, he went back to teaching. He died on 27 July 2015, mid-lecture, at IIM Shillong. There are worse ways to go.

Milestones

1931

Born in Rameswaram

Born to a modest family in a small town in Tamil Nadu. Delivered newspapers as a child to fund his education — a detail he never forgot.

1958

Joins DRDO

After graduating from MIT Chennai, Kalam joins the Defence Research and Development Organisation as a scientist. His career in aerospace begins here.

1969

Moves to ISRO

Transfers to the Indian Space Research Organisation and is appointed as project director for India's first Satellite Launch Vehicle — SLV-III.

1980

SLV-III Success

The SLV-III rocket successfully places the Rohini satellite into orbit, making India one of a small number of countries with indigenous space launch capability.

1992

Leads IGMDP

Serves as chief of the Integrated Guided Missile Development Programme. Oversees the development of Agni and Prithvi missiles, earning him the "Missile Man" title.

1997

Bharat Ratna

Awarded India's highest civilian honour — the Bharat Ratna — in recognition of his contributions to science, defence research, and national development.

1998

Pokhran-II

Plays a key role as Project Coordinator for India's second nuclear test, Operation Shakti. A defining moment in India's nuclear strategy.

2002

Becomes President

Elected as the 11th President of India. Known as the "People's President," he makes a point of meeting school children across the country throughout his term.

2007

Returns to Teaching

After his presidential term ends, he returns to academia — teaching at IIT, IIM, and universities across India — by choice, not obligation.

2015

Passes Away in Shillong

Collapses mid-lecture at IIM Shillong on 27 July 2015. He was 83. The country mourned a man who seemed to have never fully retired from serving it.

Why I Chose Him

Honestly? I chose Dr. Kalam because I grew up reading quotes attributed to him in school notebooks, on classroom walls, on the backs of buses. For a long time I didn't know much beyond the quotes — just that he was "the missile man" and that he'd been President once.

When I actually read Wings of Fire, I realised there was a real person behind all of it. Someone who grew up without much, worked incredibly hard, and still found time to write letters to students he'd never met. The part that stuck with me most wasn't any big achievement — it was the fact that when his presidential term ended, he went back to teaching. He didn't have to. He just wanted to.

I'm studying to build things on the internet. He built things that went into space and kept a country safer. The scale is completely different. But something about his approach — the curiosity, the patience, the idea that every student deserves your full attention — feels like something worth carrying into any field.

"He never stopped being a student. That is the greatest thing a teacher can be."
